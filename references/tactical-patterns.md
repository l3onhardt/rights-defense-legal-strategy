# Tactical Patterns

Use these patterns to build lawful countermeasures. Select based on facts, evidence, cost, risk, and the user's goal.

## Evidence First

Before escalation, preserve what can disappear:

- Export chat logs, emails, order records, tickets, backend screenshots, payment records, delivery records, invoices, contracts, notices, calls, and account status.
- Keep originals. Save hash/time metadata when useful. Avoid editing screenshots except for copies used for presentation.
- Build a chronology with dates, actors, exact words, amounts, and evidence file names.
- Confirm whether audio/video recording is lawful in context before using it.
- Avoid public accusations before evidence and legal wording are checked.

## Controlled Communication

Use communication to create evidence and improve leverage:

- Write calmly and specifically.
- Ask the other side to confirm facts, reasons, policies, amounts, deadlines, or responsible entity.
- Avoid admissions, emotional abuse, threats, false allegations, and irreversible waivers.
- Use "please provide the legal/contractual/platform basis" when challenged by large companies, platforms, employers, or institutions.
- Set reasonable deadlines and preserve proof of delivery.

## Pressure Ladder

Escalate step by step:

1. Private clarification and evidence preservation
2. Written notice to responsible party
3. Internal complaint, supervisor, platform appeal, or customer service escalation
4. Formal demand letter or response letter
5. Regulator, industry association, consumer/labor/market/data/financial complaint
6. Mediation or arbitration where available
7. Litigation, administrative reconsideration/litigation, criminal report, or enforcement route

At each step, ask: does this create evidence, increase lawful pressure, reduce cost, or move the user closer to the desired outcome?

## Letter and Message Structure

For demand or response text:

1. Identify parties and matter.
2. State verified facts with dates and evidence.
3. Cite contract/platform rule/legal basis only after verification.
4. State the user's demand.
5. Set a deadline.
6. Reserve rights without overthreatening.
7. Keep tone firm, short, and usable in court or complaint files.

## Complaint Strategy

A good complaint should identify:

- Correct respondent and responsible entity.
- Correct regulator/platform/channel.
- Specific facts and evidence.
- Specific violated rule or suspected violation.
- Clear request: refund, restoration, correction, investigation, penalty, mediation, written explanation, deletion, payment, performance.
- Attachments list.

Do not file random complaints. Misrouted complaints waste time and may reveal strategy.

## Settlement Strategy

When settlement is possible:

- Define money, time, performance, deletion/restoration, confidentiality, non-disparagement, tax/invoice, default liability, release scope, and evidence handling.
- Avoid broad waivers that kill unknown claims unless compensation is worth it.
- Avoid admitting fault unless strategically necessary and reviewed.
- Confirm who signs for companies and whether authority is valid.

## Risk Controls

Flag these risks early:

- Defamation or reputation infringement from public posts.
- Privacy or personal information violations.
- Illegal debt collection, threats, coercion, harassment, or extortion.
- Evidence contamination, forged screenshots, deleted originals.
- Missed limitation, appeal, objection, arbitration, or platform deadlines.
- Signing settlement, receipt, resignation, compensation confirmation, guarantee, confession, or waiver under pressure.

When risk is high, give immediate do-not-do guidance before the full plan.
