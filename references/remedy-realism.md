# Remedy Realism

Use this file when deciding whether a proposed legal, platform, complaint, negotiation, arbitration, litigation, administrative, or public-pressure route can actually deliver the user's goal.

The core question is: even if the user is right, what can this route realistically produce, at what cost, with what proof, and under what opponent response?

## 1. Remedy Reality Matrix

Use this matrix before recommending a route:

```markdown
| route | who decides | can compel? | possible remedy | prerequisites | proof needed | time/cost | opponent response | enforcement reality | user downside | settlement value |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
```

Do not confuse these outcomes:

- legal entitlement
- complaint acceptance
- mediation pressure
- administrative investigation or penalty
- platform restoration/refund
- court/arbitration judgment or award
- enforceable collection
- apology/deletion/correction
- practical settlement

A route can be legally correct but strategically weak if it cannot compel the user's actual goal or costs more than the dispute is worth.

## 2. Forum Power Levels

Classify each route by power level:

```text
level 0: information only; helps the user understand rules but creates no pressure
level 1: record-building; creates written evidence or a complaint record
level 2: mediation/soft pressure; may prompt response but cannot compel remedy
level 3: regulator/platform/internal authority; can investigate, restore, punish, freeze, correct, or escalate within scope
level 4: adjudicative authority; court/arbitration/admin reconsideration/litigation can issue binding result within jurisdiction
level 5: enforcement authority; can enforce an existing effective judgment/award/document where conditions are met
```

Use lower-power routes deliberately when they build evidence, create admissions, or set up higher-power routes. Do not sell them as final solutions.

## 3. Expected Value Check

Before escalation, estimate:

```text
best realistic outcome:
most likely outcome:
worst credible outcome:
amount or non-money value at stake:
time to result:
cash cost:
attention/emotional cost:
retaliation/reputation risk:
evidence improvement from taking the step:
settlement leverage created:
```

A user-friendly answer sometimes says: “You may have a claim, but the current expected value is weak unless we first fix evidence or narrow the demand.”

## 4. Settlement Design

When settlement is possible, define:

- money, refund, compensation, rent/deposit return, salary/payment, performance, delivery, account restoration, deletion/correction, apology, confidentiality, non-disparagement, invoice/tax, timing, default liability, authority to sign, release scope, evidence handling, and payment method
- what claims are released and what claims are preserved
- what happens if one side defaults
- whether public posts, complaints, arbitration/litigation, platform tickets, or regulator filings will be withdrawn, paused, or preserved
- whether broad waivers are worth the compensation

Do not let the user sign broad releases, admissions, resignation confirmations, debt acknowledgments, guarantee documents, or notarized instruments without understanding the downside.

## 5. Go / No-Go Rules

Recommend **go** when:

- decisive facts are provable
- the chosen forum/channel can grant or pressure the desired remedy
- cost and retaliation risk are proportionate
- the action improves evidence or settlement leverage even if it fails
- the opponent's best response has a prepared answer

Recommend **build first** when:

- the right exists but evidence is thin
- the opponent's likely defense is unanswered
- the correct respondent or forum is unclear
- a careless complaint/demand may reveal strategy
- a narrower factual confirmation request would improve the case

Recommend **settle defensively or stop** when:

- expected recovery is lower than cost/risk
- the user has serious bad facts or admissions
- the opponent can counterclaim or retaliate cheaply
- the forum cannot grant meaningful relief
- escalation creates criminal, administrative, defamation, privacy, or business reputation risk disproportionate to the goal
