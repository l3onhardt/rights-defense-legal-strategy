# Adversarial Case Harness

Use this harness whenever the dispute is meaningful, contested, expensive, public, time-sensitive, procedural, or likely to involve a lawyer, company legal department, landlord, employer, platform, regulator, court, arbitration body, police, or administrative agency.

The purpose is not to be neutral for neutrality's sake. The purpose is to serve the user by discovering the opponent's best case before the opponent does damage with it.

## 1. Mandatory Trigger

Run at least a compact version of this harness before recommending:

- formal notice, demand letter, lawyer-letter response, complaint, reporting, arbitration, litigation, administrative reconsideration/litigation, criminal report, public posting, media escalation, settlement, resignation, waiver, guarantee, admission, or apology
- any action where the other side has meaningful resources, procedure, evidence, control, reputation leverage, or retaliation ability
- any matter involving housing, employment, platform accounts, merchant funds, contracts, debt, equity, administrative pressure, family/personal safety, criminal risk, or large money

For quick low-stakes wording, use only the Draft Stress Test and Failure Mode Checklist.

## 2. Anti-Echo-Chamber Rule

Before final advice, answer these questions:

```text
what does the user want?
what facts does the user need a third party to believe?
what facts can the user prove now?
what will the other side deny or reframe?
what would a neutral decision-maker care about more than the user's anger?
what is the cheapest next action that improves proof or leverage?
```

Never present a strategy as strong merely because it is morally satisfying. If the plan needs evidence, forum power, or timing that the user does not have, say so.

## 3. Claim / Route / Defense / Proof Matrix

Use this matrix to stop vague “维权” from becoming generic escalation.

```markdown
| claim/route | what must be proven | current user evidence | opponent's strongest defense | missing proof | likely decision-maker concern | realistic remedy | confidence | next proof action |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
```

Guidance:

- `claim/route` can be contract claim, tort/privacy claim, labor arbitration demand, consumer complaint, platform appeal, market supervision complaint, rent deposit claim, administrative reconsideration, litigation, criminal report, mediation, or negotiated settlement.
- `what must be proven` should use verified law/rules where available. If unverified, label it as provisional.
- `current user evidence` should distinguish originals from screenshots, hearsay, summaries, and assumptions.
- `opponent's strongest defense` should be written as if the opponent has competent counsel.
- `missing proof` should become the next action list.
- `realistic remedy` must be something the chosen forum can actually grant or influence.

## 4. Counterparty Model

Model the opposing side as a rational actor, not a cartoon villain.

```text
actors and decision authority:
what they control:
what they want:
what they fear:
what they can prove:
what they can plausibly deny:
their strongest legal/contractual/platform argument:
their strongest factual argument:
their strongest procedural argument:
likely delay tactic:
likely counter-demand or counterclaim:
likely settlement range or face-saving exit:
retaliation risk:
```

Common counterparty moves:

- ignore informal messages and wait for proof of escalation
- deny receipt, authority, agency, fault, causation, amount, or promised terms
- rely on contract wording, platform rules, company policies, limitation periods, jurisdiction clauses, arbitration clauses, acceptance records, inspection records, or prior acknowledgments
- say the user's evidence is incomplete, edited, out of context, illegally obtained, or not connected to the defendant/respondent
- offer a partial refund/compensation with broad waiver
- delay until platform appeal, objection, limitation, or evidence deadlines pass
- file first, report first, complain first, or create a record that frames the user as unreasonable
- threaten defamation, privacy infringement, breach of confidentiality, malicious complaint, or business reputation harm

## 5. Decision-Maker Model

Identify who can actually change the outcome.

```text
decision-maker/forum:
can it compel the remedy or only mediate/pressure?
jurisdiction/scope:
procedural prerequisites:
materials required:
proof standard or practical proof expectation:
what facts matter most:
what facts are irrelevant/noisy:
common rejection reasons:
time/cost/enforcement reality:
what wording/evidence improves credibility:
```

Decision-maker examples:

- court: jurisdiction, cause of action, parties, admissible evidence, burden of proof, limitation, remedy, enforceability, litigation cost
- labor arbitration: employment relationship, wage/social insurance/overtime evidence, limitation, arbitration request, employer defenses, award enforceability
- regulator: statutory authority, complaint scope, whether it can order correction/mediate/investigate/punish, evidence packet, local discretion
- platform reviewer: platform terms, appeal window, account/order/ticket data, rule category, restoration/refund power, internal escalation route
- police/public security: whether facts meet criminal/public-order threshold, evidence of threats/fraud/violence, civil-criminal boundary, risk of false accusation
- mediator/community/industry body: voluntary pressure, reputation, settlement range, proof clarity, face-saving exit

Do not treat a complaint channel as useful merely because it exists. Ask whether it can grant, pressure, or only record.

## 6. Response Tree

For each proposed action, create at least a small response tree:

```text
if they comply:
if they ignore:
if they deny facts:
if they cite contract/rule:
if they offer partial settlement:
if they threaten counteraction:
if they file/complain/report first:
if new evidence appears against user:
```

A strong plan tells the user what to do on the second move, not only the first message.

## 7. Draft Stress Test

Before writing any message, letter, complaint, or public statement, check:

- Does it admit liability, waive rights, or concede a disputed fact?
- Does it overstate law, threaten illegally, defame, expose private information, or make unsupported accusations?
- Does it ask for a remedy that the recipient or forum can grant?
- Does it preserve evidence of delivery and response?
- Does it force the other side to confirm a fact, basis, amount, authority, deadline, or decision-maker?
- Would the text still look credible if read by a judge, arbitrator, regulator, police officer, platform reviewer, employer, or mediator?
- Does it leave a settlement exit instead of cornering the other side into escalation?

Use firm, factual, narrow wording. Avoid theatrical legal threats.

## 8. Decisive Leverage / “杀死比赛” Test

A decisive move exists only if several of these are true:

```text
objective evidence proves the key fact:
verified rule/forum supports the requested remedy:
opponent's best defense has a clear answer:
opponent faces lawful downside from refusal:
user's own downside is controlled:
action is timely:
remedy is realistic and valuable:
settlement exit is available:
```

False positives:

- “I am right” but cannot prove the key fact.
- Many complaint channels exist but none can compel the desired remedy.
- The law may support the user but amount, time, or enforcement cost makes victory hollow.
- Public pressure may feel powerful but creates defamation/privacy/reputation risk.
- The opponent is morally ugly but has a valid contract, procedure, or evidence defense.
- The user can start litigation but the opponent can counterclaim or use delay more cheaply.

If the test fails, say: **现在还没有杀死比赛的点**. Then prescribe one of these:

- collect objective evidence
- force written confirmation
- narrow the demand
- choose a forum with real authority
- reduce legal/reputational risk
- improve settlement terms
- wait for the opponent to make a mistake while preserving deadlines
- stop escalation and settle defensively if expected value is negative

## 9. Failure Mode Checklist

Check these before final strategy:

- wrong respondent or no proof of responsible entity
- wrong forum/channel or missing prerequisite
- limitation/appeal/objection/platform deadline risk
- arbitration/jurisdiction clause not checked
- evidence edited, incomplete, illegally obtained, or missing originals
- user's prior message created admission, waiver, settlement, or bad faith record
- remedy cannot be granted by chosen channel
- public statement risks defamation/privacy/business reputation liability
- complaint may reveal strategy without pressure
- opponent has cheaper delay path
- user's cost exceeds likely recovery
- retaliation risk is not controlled
- settlement release is too broad
- criminal/admin route is being misused for civil pressure
